ALL ABOUT ILOILO

History of Iloilo

Iloilo is a city in the Philippines that traces its history back to pre-colonial times as the center of Madja-as. The city originated from the town of Oton, an outpost founded by Spanish conquistador Miguel Lopez de Legazpi1. Raids by Chinese and Moro pirates forced the relocation of the outpost to Villa Rica de Arevalo. Iloilo was the second Spanish colonial center after Cebu in the Philippine Islands, and was founded in 1566. It was one of the royal Spanish cities in the Philippines in the Spanish East Indies.

More about Iloilo City

Iloilo is renowned not only for its picturesque landscapes but also for its rich culinary heritage and vibrant cultural traditions. The province's cuisine is a delightful tapestry of flavors, reflecting a fusion of indigenous, Spanish, and Chinese influences. Seafood plays a prominent role, given the province's coastal location, with dishes like La Paz Batchoy, a noodle soup enriched with pork offal, chicharrón (crispy pork cracklings), and a savory broth, standing out as a local favorite. Freshly caught seafood, such as prawns, crabs, and various fish, graces many dining tables, showcasing the region's bountiful marine resources. The people of Iloilo take great pride in their distinct culture, which is marked by a strong sense of community and hospitality. Festivals are integral to their cultural identity, with events like the Dinagyang Festival standing out as a major celebration. Dinagyang is a vibrant and dynamic festival that pays homage to the region's indigenous heritage and Catholic traditions through colorful street performances, music, and religious processions. Traditional arts and crafts are also deeply embedded in Iloilo's culture. The province is known for its intricate handwoven textiles, such as the hablon and patadyong, showcasing the artistry and skill of local weavers. These fabrics are not only functional but also serve as a source of pride, preserving age-old weaving techniques passed down through generations. Iloilo's cultural richness is further highlighted by its colonial-era churches, ancestral houses, and historical landmarks. The Miag-ao Church, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, stands as a testament to the region's deep Catholic heritage and architectural prowess. In essence, Iloilo's food and culture intertwine to create a captivating and immersive experience for those who venture into this charming province. The culinary delights and cultural expressions of Iloilo contribute to the province's unique identity, making it a destination that satisfies both the palate and the soul.


TOURIST ATTRACTIONS

Miag-ao Church: A UNESCO site blending Baroque-Romanesque architecture with indigenous motifs.
Islas de Gigantes: Captivating islands, historically rumored as pirate treasure hiding spots during the Spanish era.
Jaro Cathedral: Dating to 1863, the first Philippine cathedral dedicated to Our Lady of the Candles.

Iloilo Architecture

Their architecture is a captivating fusion of diverse styles, blending indigenous motifs, colonial influences, and contemporary designs. These structures collectively narrate a unique chapter in the province's vibrant history, showcasing the richness of Iloilo's architectural heritage.

The most famous festival in Iloilo, Dinagyang, originated in the 1960s to honor the Santo Niño and commemorate the arrival of Malay settlers, this serves as a vibrant celebration in Iloilo, showcasing the region's rich cultural heritage through dynamic street performances and religious processions.

Fun facts about Iloilo City

Fun facts about Iloilo include its endearing nickname as the "Heart of the Philippines," a nod to its central location and rich cultural tapestry. Iloilo City, in particular, boasts several charming monikers such as the "City of Love," the "Queen City of the South," and the "Museum City." Adding to its allure, the city serves as a major fishing port with a bustling airport. Roaming through its compact urban core, one can explore various plaza and market centers.
